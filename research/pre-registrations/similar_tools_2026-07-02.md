# Survey: Deployed AI-image-detector UX, provenance channels, calibration practice, and 2025–2026 white-box literature

Date: 2026-07-02
Scope: research only, no code changes. Written to inform the verdict/report UI design for a white-box (NumPy/SciPy + sklearn LogisticRegression only) AI-image detector with in-scope AUC ~0.75–0.88 (2022-era gens) degrading to ~0.60–0.65 on cross-lab frontier gens (GPT-Image, Gemini/Nano Banana, Midjourney v7, Imagen-4).

## 1. Deployed product practices — verdict/report design survey

### 1.1 Hive Moderation (Hive AI Detector)
- Two-head model: head 1 = binary AI-vs-not with a **confidence score** (0–1 / %), head 2 = generator attribution (which model likely produced it: Midjourney, DALL-E 3, SDXL, Flux, etc.).
- Explicitly designed for **policy thresholds rather than a hard verdict** — Hive's own docs frame the score as something integrators use to set a "require human review above X%" gate rather than auto-labeling content, i.e., built-in support for an abstention/escalation band.
- Ships a "detailed anomaly breakdown" report calling out lighting inconsistencies, pixel inconsistencies, texture anomalies — an explainability layer on top of the score.
- Independent 2026 benchmarking cited ~94% accuracy across Midjourney v6/DALL-E 3/SDXL, but this is an easier "2022–2024-era" corpus mix, consistent with my own in-scope numbers, not a frontier-gen number.
- Sources: [Hive AI Detector Review 2026](https://aidetector.ac/reviews/hive/), [Hive AI Detector: Features, Accuracy & Limitations](https://hastewire.com/blog/hive-ai-detector-features-accuracy-and-limitations-explained), [Hive Moderation product page](https://hivemoderation.com/ai-generated-content-detection/installation-complete), [Hive on NVIDIA build catalog](https://build.nvidia.com/hive/ai-generated-image-detection/modelcard)

### 1.2 Sightengine
- Returns a continuous **0–1 confidence score** ("higher = more likely AI-generated") plus **per-generator sub-scores** (Flux, GPT-Image, Nano Banana/Nano Banana Pro, Imagen, Midjourney, SD 1.x/2.x/XL/SD3, Firefly, Qwen, Ideogram, Recraft, Reve, SeeDream, Kling, Wan, GAN families).
- Explicitly documents that detection is **pixel-only**: metadata, EXIF, C2PA, and invisible watermarks are ignored by the core `genai` model, so provenance signals are a *separate*, additive product surface, not fused into the pixel score. This is a notable design precedent for keeping the two channels (pixel forensics vs. provenance) legible and separately reported rather than blended into one number.
- Public-facing "Which one is AI?" game and "AI or Not" quiz double as calibrated expectation-setting for end users (showing humans also struggle), a soft honesty device.
- Sources: [AI-generated Image Detection API docs](https://sightengine.com/docs/ai-generated-image-detection), [AI Image Detector product page](https://sightengine.com/detect-ai-generated-images), [Which one is AI, which one is real? game](https://sightengine.com/which-image-is-ai), [Human abilities to spot AI images](https://sightengine.com/human-abilities-to-spot-ai-images)

### 1.3 Illuminarty
- Returns confidence scores **plus spatial localization** — highlights the image regions that most increased AI-probability (heatmap-style explainability), and gives a **per-generator-family breakdown** including a "hand-drawn/human artist" bucket for art-style false positives.
- Independent academic probing (western-blot-image study) found Illuminarty gives **high-variance, high-median scores on both AI and authentic images** (median 86.2 for AI, 81.1 for authentic in that domain-shifted test set) — a concrete illustration of poor cross-domain calibration when a detector is pushed outside its training distribution. A good cautionary data point for my own cross-lab drop.
- Sources: [Illuminarty product](https://illuminarty.ai/en/), [Illuminarty image detection page](https://illuminarty.ai/en/image/ai-generated-image-detection.html), [AI Detectors are Poor Western Blot Classifiers (arXiv 2407.10308)](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2407.10308)

### 1.4 Winston AI
- Claims very high internal accuracy (99.98%-style marketing numbers are common across this whole product category for *text* detection); independent estimates put real-world accuracy meaningfully lower (~87–92% for text; no solid independent number found for its image detector specifically).
- Documented failure mode: **works well on fully-synthetic content, degrades sharply on hybrid/edited/"mostly real" content** — directly analogous to my FLUX/SD-lineage cross-pipeline degradation (0.66–0.78) and frontier-gen collapse (0.57–0.65).
- Sources: [Winston AI Review 2026 — False Flags and Trust](https://blog.aibusted.com/winston-ai-detector-review-2026/), [Best AI Image Detector 2026 — Winston](https://gowinston.ai/best-ai-image-detector-2/)

### 1.5 TrueMedia.org (shut down Jan 2025, but the methodology is still instructive)
- Used **ensemble-of-detectors aggregation** ("dozens of industry and academic models") specifically to reduce false positives and boost reliability, rather than trusting any single model's score — validates a multi-signal fusion approach (which is architecturally close to what V3 already does: LR core + Mahalanobis envelope + SynthID matched filter + operator-response).
- Presented a **binary verdict with explanatory insights** attached (not just a number) — i.e., verdict + "why" narrative, not verdict alone.
- Open-sourced its models/code on shutdown: [github.com/truemediaorg/ml-models](https://github.com/truemediaorg/ml-models), [github.com/truemediaorg/deepfake-app](https://github.com/truemediaorg/deepfake-app)
- Sources: [TrueMedia.org newsroom launch post](https://www.truemedia.org/post/free-deepfake-detector-to-help-newsrooms-across-the-country), [Open-source deepfake detection models with TrueMedia.org (Medium)](https://automata88.medium.com/open-source-deepfake-detection-models-from-truemedia-org-29f9ce59882d)

### 1.6 FotoForensics (Error Level Analysis)
- Deliberately **does not give a verdict at all** — it exposes a raw diagnostic visualization (ELA heat map) and leaves interpretation to a trained analyst, explicitly warning "ELA is simply a tool; it does not tell us if a photo was manipulated." It documents known failure modes (multi-resave images collapse ELA to uniform black = false negative).
- Recommends **cross-checking with a second, independently-implemented method** before trusting any single forensic signal — directly applicable precedent for framing the LR-core + Mahalanobis + SynthID-filter + operator-response as independent corroborating channels in the UI, shown separately rather than only as one fused score.
- Sources: [FotoForensics & ELA — FlatEarth.ws](https://flatearth.ws/fotoforensics), [Error Level Analysis — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Error_level_analysis), [Infosec Institute ELA guide](https://www.infosecinstitute.com/resources/digital-forensics/error-level-analysis-detect-image-manipulation/)

### 1.7 InVID/WeVerify (journalist/fact-checker tool)
- Presents a **toolkit of 8 independent forensic filters** in a "Forensic tab" (magnifier/deep-zoom, ELA-like filters, etc.) rather than a single verdict — puts the interpretation burden on a trained user (journalists/fact-checkers), and gates the most powerful features behind a professional-verification wall to prevent misuse/over-trust by the general public.
- Precedent for **role-gating interpretive power**: give the public a simple verdict+confidence, but expose the full forensic breakdown only in an "expert mode" or to registered/professional users.
- Sources: [WeVerify plugin](https://weverify.eu/verification-plugin/), [InVID — Bellingcat toolkit](https://bellingcat.gitbook.io/toolkit/more/all-tools/invid), [CEDMO InVID-WeVerify guide](https://cedmohub.eu/invid-weverify-verification-plugin/)

### 1.8 Reality Defender (enterprise deepfake detection)
- Publishes explicit **anti-marketing-hype guidance**: warns buyers that "a 99% benchmark score is never guaranteed in production" and that vendors should be asked for the **false-positive rate at production volume**, not just headline accuracy, because "a system optimized solely for sensitivity will generate false positives at scale, leading to alert fatigue."
- Cites the DeepFake-Eval-2024 academic benchmark (see §5) showing leading commercial detectors only reach ~78% accuracy on in-the-wild content vs. much higher lab-benchmark numbers — this is the single best "honesty precedent" found for what I should say publicly about my own 0.60–0.65 cross-lab AUC vs. 0.88 in-scope AUC.
- Recommended reporting elements: architecture transparency (how many models/signals, how they differ), false-positive rate at scale, and how multi-model outputs are reconciled into a consensus verdict rather than presented as one opaque number.
- Source: [Why Lab Benchmarks Fail Real-World Deepfake Detection](https://www.realitydefender.com/insights/lab-benchmarks-ineffective-in-deepfake-detection)

### 1.9 Decopy / IsItAI (consumer-grade free tools)
- Typical pattern: **verdict + 0–100% confidence + likely-generator guess + NSFW flag**, with a short bullet-point "what tipped it off" explanation (lighting, texture uniformity, pattern repetition).
- Independent testing found real accuracy far below marketing claims (e.g., one comparison found Decopy at ~18–65% accuracy depending on test set, with a ~6.8% false-positive rate on real photos, and near-0% detection on some 2025/2026-era generators like Wan v2.6) — another concrete illustration of the frontier-gen collapse phenomenon I'm already seeing, and a caution that consumer tools without disclosed methodology should not be used as an accuracy bar.
- Sources: [Decopy AI Image Detector](https://decopy.ai/ai-image-detector/), [Decopy AI Detector Review — AI Detector Arena](https://aidetectarena.com/detectors/decopy), [Is It AI? image detector](https://isitai.com/ai-image-detector)

### 1.10 Cross-cutting UX pattern observations relevant to the product
- **No major deployed tool uses a strict binary verdict without a numeric score attached.** Score-plus-label is universal.
- **Abstention/"inconclusive" bands are common in spirit but rarely explicit as a labeled UI state** — most tools implement it implicitly via "score near 50% = ambiguous" rather than a formally named third bucket. This is a gap/opportunity: an explicitly labeled "Insufficient evidence / outside detector's validated scope" verdict band would be more honest than anything surveyed and differentiates the product.
- **Per-generator / per-scope breakdown is standard** among the more credible tools (Hive, Sightengine, Illuminarty) — validates the "per-generator breakdown mandatory" evaluation discipline as something to surface in the *product*, not just internal eval.
- **Explainability ("why flagged") is treated as a first-class UI element**, not an afterthought, across Hive, Illuminarty, Decopy, InVID. Given my features are literally interpretable (spectral slope, VAE-grid periodicity, Mahalanobis distance-from-real-envelope, SynthID matched-filter response, denoiser-residual ratios), there is a structural advantage: I can show the actual feature values / z-scores driving the verdict, something black-box competitors can only approximate with saliency heuristics.
- **Nobody surveyed publishes a rigorous, symmetric cross-lab AUC number in-product.** Reality Defender is the only vendor found explicitly coaching *buyers* to demand this, but does not appear to publish its own. This is a genuine whitespace: publishing calibrated, honest, generator-stratified AUC (e.g., "0.88 on 2022-era generators, 0.60–0.65 on frontier closed-lab generators as of July 2026") would be unusual in this market and could be a credibility differentiator rather than a liability, provided it's framed correctly (see §3 recommendations).

## 2. Provenance/metadata channel — C2PA, SynthID, IPTC (2026 state)

### 2.1 C2PA Content Credentials
- **c2pa-python** is the official Python binding (wraps c2pa-rs, the Rust reference implementation). Requires Python >=3.10, installable via `pip install c2pa-python`. Provides read/validate of C2PA manifests embedded in JPEG/PNG/etc., and can extract manifest assertions (author, edit history, ingredients, generator claims) and validation status. CPU-only, no GPU/deep-learning dependency — fits the white-box/CPU constraint as a *provenance* channel (distinct from a pixel-forensics feature).
  - Repo: [github.com/contentauth/c2pa-python](https://github.com/contentauth/c2pa-python), example repo: [github.com/contentauth/c2pa-python-example](https://github.com/contentauth/c2pa-python-example), docs: [opensource.contentauthenticity.org/docs/c2pa-python](https://opensource.contentauthenticity.org/docs/c2pa-python/), package: [pypi.org/project/c2pa-python](https://pypi.org/project/c2pa-python/)
  - I could not confirm the exact Python field names for trust/tamper-validation status from the docs pages fetched (rate-limited/partial content); before building the integration, read `examples/read.py` directly from the repo and the "Using the Python library" doc page for the concrete API (`c2pa.Reader`, manifest `.validation_status` list, etc. — verify the exact class/method names in the current repo rather than relying on this note).
- **2026 adoption state (verified via multiple independent sources)**: As of May 19, 2026, **OpenAI joined the C2PA steering committee** and began embedding both C2PA Content Credentials *and* Google's SynthID watermark in generated images — a major convergence event. Adobe Firefly, DALL-E 3, Sora, Google Imagen, and Stability's hosted API already embedded C2PA credentials as of early 2026. **Midjourney does NOT embed C2PA as of early 2026** — an important gap, since Midjourney is one of the frontier-gen weak spots (v7 in the cross-lab set).
  - Sources: [OpenAI: Advancing content provenance for a safer, more transparent AI ecosystem](https://openai.com/index/advancing-content-provenance/), [TechCrunch: OpenAI is making it easier to check if an image was made by their models](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/19/openai-is-making-it-easier-to-check-if-an-image-was-made-by-their-models/), [C2PA Viewer: OpenAI and Google align on C2PA and SynthID](https://c2paviewer.com/articles/openai-google-c2pa-synthid-2026), [help.openai.com C2PA/SynthID article](https://help.openai.com/en/articles/8912793-c2pa-and-synthid-in-openai-generated-images)
- **Critical limitation for a benchmark/product**: C2PA presence is trivially strippable (re-save, screenshot, most social platforms strip metadata on upload) and its **absence proves nothing** — a real photo with no C2PA and an AI image with stripped C2PA look identical on this channel. It is a **high-precision, low-recall auxiliary signal**: if present and valid, strong evidence; if absent, uninformative. This matches my framing ("valuable in the product, not usable in benchmark training") — confirmed as sound.

### 2.2 IPTC Digital Source Type (XMP field, feeds into C2PA assertions)
- IPTC's `DigitalSourceType` NewsCodes vocabulary now has stable values: `trainedAlgorithmicMedia` ("Created using Generative AI") for fully-synthetic images, and `compositeWithTrainedAlgorithmicMedia` ("Edited with Generative AI") for inpainting/outpainting/composite edits. Google Search/Merchant Center now surface these labels directly.
  - Sources: [IPTC Digital Source Type NewsCodes](https://cv.iptc.org/newscodes/digitalsourcetype/), [IPTC news: metadata guidance for AI-generated "synthetic media"](https://iptc.org/news/iptc-publishes-metadata-guidance-for-ai-generated-synthetic-media/), [Google Search extracting composite/trainedAlgorithmicMedia types](https://www.seroundtable.com/google-search-new-iptc-digital-source-compositewithtrainedalgorithmicmedia-37694.html)
- Same strippability caveat as C2PA applies (XMP metadata is trivially removed on re-save/screenshot).

### 2.3 SynthID (Google DeepMind watermark)
- By May 2026, **>100 billion pieces of content watermarked** (image/video/audio/text), shipped by default in every Google generative product (Gemini, Imagen, Veo, Lyria) and now adopted by NVIDIA, OpenAI, Kakao, ElevenLabs.
- Google launched a public **SynthID Detector** web portal for verification, and announced (Google I/O 2026) that **C2PA verification + SynthID detection are being built natively into Google Search and Chrome**.
- This directly matters to the "SynthID matched-filter (FFT carrier)" V3 feature: it confirms SynthID is an increasingly dominant, expanding-scope watermark (not a niche Imagen-only thing), which should make the matched-filter feature *more* valuable over time as adoption spreads to more labs' outputs, though the matched filter targets the pixel-level FFT carrier, which is a different (and complementary) channel from the official verification API/portal.
- Sources: [SynthID — Google DeepMind](https://deepmind.google/models/synthid/), [Google blog: SynthID Detector](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/products/google-synthid-ai-content-detector/), [InfoQ: Google expands SynthID adoption, previews Content Detection API](https://www.infoq.com/news/2026/05/google-synthid-content-detection/)

### 2.4 Other watermark carriers
- **Meta's Stable Signature** (rooted in the LDM decoder weights, not post-hoc): detects the origin of a generated image even after cropping to 10% of content, claimed 90%+ accuracy at FPR < 1e-6. However, **published attacks remove it via decoder fine-tuning** ("Stable Signature is Unstable"), so treat as a soft signal, not ground truth. Sources: [Meta AI blog: Stable Signature](https://ai.meta.com/blog/stable-signature-watermarking-generative-ai/), [arXiv 2303.15435 original paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.15435), [OpenReview: Stable Signature is Unstable](https://openreview.net/forum?id=zqo2eKjSWH)
- **Microsoft InvisMark**: invisible, robust watermarking for AI-image provenance (arXiv 2411.07795) — another carrier family besides SynthID worth being aware of if the matched-filter approach is extended.
- General watermark robustness literature (2025–2026) shows an active attack/defense arms race — "Editing Away the Evidence" (arXiv 2603.12949) and "Brittleness of AI-Generated Image Watermarking" (arXiv 2408.10446) both show common robust-watermark schemes fail under diffusion-based re-editing/paraphrasing. Treat any watermark-presence signal as probabilistic corroboration, never sole ground truth.

### 2.5 Recommendation on the provenance channel
Build a **separate, clearly-labeled "Provenance check" panel** (C2PA manifest present/valid + IPTC DigitalSourceType + SynthID-detector-API result if integrated) shown *alongside*, not blended into, the pixel-forensics LR score. This mirrors Sightengine's explicit "pixel-only, metadata ignored" design choice and FotoForensics'/InVID's "independent corroborating tools" philosophy — and is honest about the channel's real character (high precision when present, zero information when absent/stripped).

## 3. Selective prediction / calibration for a modest-AUC classifier

### 3.1 Core recommended technique: Platt/isotonic calibration + conformal selective classification
- Standard, well-established two-step recipe found consistently in the literature: (1) calibrate raw LR scores into well-calibrated probabilities (Platt scaling — trivial with sklearn's `CalibratedClassifierCV`, or isotonic regression given enough held-out data and no need for parametric smoothness); (2) apply a **conformal / selective-classification abstention layer** on top, which converts "the classifier said 62%" into "the classifier says AI-generated with X% coverage guarantee, or abstains."
- **Selective Conformal Risk Control (SCRC)**, arXiv 2512.12844 (2025/2026): a clean, directly-applicable framework — two-stage: (1) a selection function `g(X)` (can be as simple as margin from the decision boundary, or max-probability) decides whether to abstain; (2) conformal risk control is applied only to the *selected* (non-abstained) subset, giving **finite-sample, distribution-free coverage/risk guarantees** on the accepted predictions. Two variants: SCRC-T (exact finite-sample, joint calibration+test threshold) and SCRC-I (calibration-only, more practical, PAC-style guarantee). This is compatible with a plain LR model — no deep-learning internals required, since the selection function can be built from the LR decision score / Mahalanobis distance alone. Source: [arXiv:2512.12844](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.12844), [HTML version](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.12844v1)
- A follow-up, "A Joint Finite-Sample Certificate for Adaptive Selective Conformal Risk Control" (arXiv 2606.08517), extends this with adaptive thresholds — worth a look if the basic SCRC abstention band is too coarse in practice.
- General selective-prediction literature note (multiple sources triangulated): naive "abstain below confidence threshold T" is **known to produce poorly-calibrated accepted predictions** unless you explicitly calibrate on the accepted subset — i.e., don't just pick a raw-probability cutoff by eyeballing; use conformal calibration on a held-out calibration set that matches the real-vs-real cross-corpus gate discipline.

### 3.2 Deepfake-specific uncertainty work (mostly not directly applicable, but informative)
- "Architecture-Adaptive Uncertainty Fusion for Deepfake Detection" (arXiv 2606.06666, code at [github.com/sharmrit/cof-deepfake](https://github.com/sharmrit/cof-deepfake)) fuses 5 uncertainty sources (epistemic via MC-dropout, aleatoric, calibration-gap, conformal p-values, Mahalanobis distributional distance) via correlation-optimized weighting. **Key transferable finding**: in their cross-domain tests, "in-domain uncertainty-error correlations of 0.41–0.47 collapse by 90.7% out-of-domain," and **the *only* uncertainty source that stayed stable cross-domain was the distributional/Mahalanobis-distance signal** (-2.9% degradation vs. >90% degradation for softmax/conformal-derived sources). This is a strong, directly relevant finding for this architecture: **the existing one-class Mahalanobis "real envelope" score is exactly the kind of signal this paper finds most cross-domain-robust as an uncertainty/abstention driver** — consider using Mahalanobis distance from the pooled-real-null (not just as a feature, but as the primary abstention/selection signal) rather than raw LR probability margin.
- Most of the rest of this paper's machinery (MC-dropout, penultimate-feature Mahalanobis in a deep net) assumes a deep-learning backbone and does **not** transfer to a pure sklearn LR — noted as a rejected-approach precedent for anyone tempted to add dropout-style ensembling.

### 3.3 Practical calibration/abstention design for the product
Based on triangulating Reality Defender's honesty guidance (§1.8), Hive's "confidence-threshold gating" precedent (§1.1), and the SCRC framework (§3.1):
1. **Calibrate per-scope, not globally.** Given the eval discipline already produces per-generator AUC breakdowns (0.88 / 0.66–0.78 / 0.57–0.65), fit **separate calibration curves per scope-band** (or at minimum, a coarse "in-scope vs. frontier" indicator feature — e.g., distance-from-real-envelope or a generator-family heuristic) rather than one global Platt scaler across wildly different operating points. A single global calibration will systematically overstate confidence on frontier images.
2. **Report calibrated probability + an explicit abstention band**, e.g. a three-way verdict: "Likely AI-generated" / "Likely authentic" / "Inconclusive — outside validated detection scope," where the inconclusive band is set via conformal selective classification (SCRC) to hit a target risk level (e.g., <=10% error rate on the accepted 2/3rds of traffic) rather than an arbitrary 40–60% cutoff.
3. **Surface the AUC/scope honestly per generator-class in the report itself** (not just in a methodology page) — e.g., "This image's characteristics are most similar to the 2022-era training scope (validated AUC 0.88)" vs. "...most similar to frontier closed-lab generators (validated AUC ~0.60–0.65, treat this verdict as weak evidence only)." This operationalizes Reality Defender's "false positive rate at production volume, not headline accuracy" honesty principle and is something no surveyed competitor does explicitly.
4. Use the **Mahalanobis real-envelope distance as the primary selection/abstention signal** (per the §3.2 finding of cross-domain robustness), separate from the LR probability used for the AI/real direction.

## 4. New white-box / handcrafted features 2025–2026 (secondary priority)

Most 2025–2026 SOTA detection papers are deep-learning-based and explicitly out of scope for these constraints. The following are the closest to usable/adaptable white-box ideas found, with honest caveats:

- **"Handcrafted Feature Fusion for Reliable Detection of AI-Generated Images"** (arXiv 2601.19262, Jan 2026): benchmarks classic handcrafted features (DCT, HOG, LBP, GLCM, color histograms, wavelet descriptors) fused via gradient-boosted trees (LightGBM/XGBoost/CatBoost) on CIFAKE, reporting ROC-AUC 0.988. **Caveat**: CIFAKE is an easy, resolution-mismatched, non-JPEG-Q75-controlled benchmark; no cross-generator or JPEG-robustness ablation reported in the abstract/summary obtained. GLCM and color features are both already on my rejected list (redundant/corpus-coupled), so this paper mostly reinforces "already tried" rather than opening new ground — but LightGBM-over-LR as the final classifier (still arguably explainable via feature importances/SHAP, though not linear-white-box in the strict sense) could be worth a controlled ablation if I ever relax the strict-LR constraint. [arXiv:2601.19262](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.19262)
- **"Three Forensic Cues for JPEG AI Images"** (arXiv 2504.03191, Bergmann/Brand/Riess): three genuinely handcrafted, interpretable cues — (1) cross-color-channel correlations introduced by JPEG-AI-style neural preprocessing, (2) recompression/diminishing-distortion detection (classic double-JPEG forensics logic applied to "JPEG AI" — note: this targets images compressed with *learned* JPEG-AI codecs, not standard libjpeg Q75, so relevance to this substrate needs verification), (3) latent-space quantization signatures. Authors explicitly frame these as "interpretable for a forensic analyst." **Caveat**: I could not extract the numeric JPEG-quality-robustness table from the abstract; this needs a full-PDF read before adopting — flagged as a promising lead requiring deeper verification, not a confirmed win. [arXiv:2504.03191](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.03191)
- **"Training-free Detection of AI-generated images via Cropping Robustness"** (arXiv 2511.14030, Nov 2025): core idea — real images maintain consistent semantic/feature properties across different crops; AI-generated images show crop-instability. Described as training-free and interpretable, but the concrete implementation extracts features via **DINOv2/CLIP embeddings** (pretrained deep vision backbones), so despite being "training-free" in the sense of not fine-tuning, it is **not white-box/NumPy-only** — fails the hard constraint. Conceptually interesting (crop-consistency as a signal) but would need a from-scratch, non-deep-embedding reformulation (e.g., using classical texture/edge statistics instead of DINOv2 features) to qualify, and note this is adjacent to the already-rejected multi-scale 512+256 approach (resolution-history confound) — likely to hit the same corpus-coupling problem. [arXiv:2511.14030](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.14030)
- **"How Fragile Are Training-Free AI-Generated Image Detectors?"** (arXiv 2606.20488, June 2026) — important cautionary/validating paper: finds current training-free detectors suffer **score-direction inversions**, extreme preprocessing sensitivity, and severe degradation even at JPEG Q95 (let alone the Q75 substrate). This is strong external validation that the rigorous evaluation discipline here (real-vs-real cross-corpus rejection gate, frozen-prediction-only, per-generator breakdown) is addressing a real, well-documented failure mode in this literature, not an overcautious process. Worth citing as justification for the strictness of the gates. [arXiv:2606.20488](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.20488)
- **Autoregressive/token-grid artifacts (GPT-Image-class)**: D3QE (ICCV 2025, arXiv 2510.05891) and PRADA (arXiv 2511.20068) both exploit **codebook/quantization frequency-distribution bias** in VQ-tokenized autoregressive generators — a genuinely new artifact class not on my rejected list. However, both published implementations use a transformer/attention network on the quantization-error latent, not a closed-form statistic — **not directly white-box**, but the underlying signal (histogram of VQ-codebook index frequencies / quantization error distribution) is conceptually reducible to a classical Benford-like/chi-square goodness-of-fit statistic on codebook usage, which *would* be white-box given access to or an approximation of a codebook-index proxy from pixel statistics alone (non-trivial — GPT-Image's tokenizer isn't public). Flagged as a promising but currently-inaccessible-without-model-access research direction, not immediately implementable. [D3QE arXiv:2510.05891](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.05891) / [code](https://github.com/Zhangyr2022/D3QE), [PRADA arXiv:2511.20068](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.20068)
- **PRNU-absence as an AI-detection signal**: multiple 2025–2026 sources (including an IEEE 2025 paper "Photo Response Non-Uniformity Based AI-Generated Image Detection") confirm PRNU-based detection is an active line, claimed 75–85% standalone accuracy, and — per the general PRNU literature — the fingerprint **persists through moderate JPEG compression (quality >=50)**, so Q75 should be survivable in principle. This is genuinely white-box (classical wavelet-denoising residual + correlation), not on my rejected list explicitly (distinct from the existing "noise-cov/residual-skew" and "operator-response/denoiser-residual-ratio" features, though closely related — PRNU specifically uses **cross-image reference-pattern correlation**, not just single-image residual statistics). **Major caveat**: PRNU is fundamentally a *camera-identity* forensic tool requiring either (a) a reference pattern from multiple images of the same camera, or (b) treating "PRNU present at all vs. absent" as the AI-signal — the latter framing is exactly the kind of single-corpus-coupled feature the real-vs-real rejection gate is designed to catch (real photos from different sources/cameras will have wildly different PRNU strength depending on JPEG history, sensor, downstream processing — high risk of failing the AUC<0.65 real-vs-real gate). Flagged as worth a rigorous test *only* through the existing cross-corpus rejection discipline; the a priori risk of rejection is high given how similar it is to noise-cov (already in V3) and residual-GLCM (already rejected). Sources: [IEEE PRNU-based AI detection](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/10776638/), [Beyond PRNU (MDPI)](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/22/20/7871)

**Overall Q4 conclusion**: the 2025–2026 white-box-adjacent literature is thin. Most genuinely novel artifact classes (autoregressive quantization bias, DINOv2 crop-consistency) require deep pretrained backbones and are disqualified by the hard constraint. The most promising unexplored-and-plausibly-compliant lead is the **JPEG-AI three-cues paper** (needs full-text verification) and a **carefully-gated PRNU-absence test** (high prior probability of failing the real-vs-real gate, but cheap to test and not literally identical to anything already rejected).

## 5. Competitor accuracy reality-check (secondary priority)

- **Deepfake-Eval-2024** (arXiv 2503.02857, Partnership on AI / academic collaboration): the most rigorous, independent, in-the-wild benchmark found. 1,975 images (+ video/audio) sourced from 88 websites in 52 languages, collected from real deepfake-detection-platform users and social media in 2024 (i.e., not a curated academic dataset). **Leading commercial detectors achieved only ~78% accuracy** on this in-the-wild set, and **none reached >=90%**. Open-source SOTA models showed AUC drops of ~45% (image), ~48% (audio), ~50% (video) compared to their original academic-benchmark-reported numbers. This is the strongest available third-party evidence that commercial-vendor accuracy claims (94–99%+) do not hold up under realistic distribution shift, and gives a legitimate, citable external anchor: **"even leading commercial detectors reach only ~78% in-the-wild, with none exceeding 90%"** — my 0.88 in-scope / 0.60–0.65 cross-lab numbers are not unusually weak by this standard; they're in a comparable-to-somewhat-below-comparable range depending on scope definition. Sources: [arXiv:2503.02857](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.02857), [HTML](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.02857v1), [EmergentMind summary](https://www.emergentmind.com/topics/deepfake-eval-2024)
- **"How well are open sourced AI-generated image detection models out-of-the-box"** (arXiv 2602.07814, 2026): benchmarked 16 detection methods across multiple datasets/generators. The best detector (**Community-Forensics**, trained on 4,803 generator outputs, arXiv 2411.04125) achieved **75.0% mean accuracy / 82.1% median**, the worst detector only 37.5% — a 37-point spread among "state of the art" open models. On **frontier commercial generators specifically (Flux Dev, Firefly v4, Midjourney v7), average accuracy across ALL tested detectors collapsed to 18–30%** (Flux Dev 21%, Firefly v4 18%). Cross-dataset rank correlation between different eval sets ranged from 0.01 to 0.87 — **no universal best detector**, rankings are highly dataset-dependent. This is a strong, directly comparable external validation that **frontier-generator detection collapse (my 0.57–0.65 range) is an industry-wide phenomenon, not a weakness specific to a white-box/no-deep-learning approach** — even large deep-learning ensembles trained on thousands of generators collapse to near-chance on the newest closed-lab models. Sources: [arXiv:2602.07814 PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.07814), [HTML](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.07814v1), [Community Forensics paper](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.04125v2)
- **Caution on marketing/consumer-benchmark sites**: "AI Detector Arena" (aidetectarena.com) reported Hive at "100% accuracy, 0% FPR" — but on inspection this was based on a test set of just **9 images**, essentially noise-level statistically and not a credible number. Treat any consumer comparison-site benchmark with an N below ~100 per generator as marketing, not evidence; the Deepfake-Eval-2024 and arXiv 2602.07814 studies above are the credible references. Decopy AI's own independent review numbers swung from 18% to 65% correctness depending on which reviewer's test set was used — further illustrating how unstable/gameable small-N public benchmarks are. Sources: [AI Detector Arena — Hive](https://aidetectarena.com/detectors/hive), [Decopy AI Review — Gold Penguin](https://goldpenguin.org/blog/undetectable-ai-vs-decopy-ai-image-detection/)

**Bottom line for product claims**: I can honestly and defensibly say the detector's cross-lab frontier performance (AUC 0.57–0.65) is "in line with the best published open-source ensembles' 18–30% raw accuracy on the same generator class" and the in-scope 0.88 AUC is "above the ~78% in-the-wild accuracy ceiling reported for leading commercial vendors in the most rigorous independent benchmark available (Deepfake-Eval-2024)" — both are citable, verified claims, not spin.

## 6. Synthesis — concrete recommendations for the web UI

1. **Three-way verdict, not binary**: "Likely AI-generated" / "Likely authentic" / "Inconclusive (outside validated detection scope)" — set the inconclusive band via conformal selective classification (SCRC, §3.1) calibrated to a target risk level, using the Mahalanobis real-envelope distance as the primary abstention driver (found to be the most cross-domain-stable uncertainty signal in the deepfake-uncertainty literature, §3.2).
2. **Report calibrated probability, not the raw LR decision score** — Platt-scale or isotonic-calibrate, ideally per scope-band, following the universal score-plus-confidence pattern across every credible surveyed tool (§1.10).
3. **Show per-feature/per-channel evidence, not just the fused number** — leverage the fact that every one of the V3 features is individually interpretable (VAE-grid periodicity strength, spectral slope deviation, Mahalanobis distance from real-envelope, SynthID matched-filter response, denoiser-residual ratios). This is a structural advantage over every black-box competitor surveyed and matches the "explainability as first-class UI" pattern seen in Hive/Illuminarty/Decopy/InVID (§1.10).
4. **Separate the pixel-forensics verdict from a provenance-check panel** (C2PA validity, IPTC DigitalSourceType, SynthID-detector-API result if integrated) — following Sightengine's explicit "pixel-only, metadata separate" design and the fundamental epistemic difference (provenance = high-precision-when-present/uninformative-when-absent; pixel forensics = probabilistic always). Do not fuse these into one score.
5. **Publish generator-scope-stratified AUC directly in the report/methodology page**, framed the way Reality Defender coaches buyers to demand (§1.8) and Deepfake-Eval-2024/arXiv 2602.07814 validate as industry-standard-or-better (§5) — this is close to unique in the market and is a credibility play, not a liability, as long as it's paired with the selective-prediction abstention mechanism so low-confidence frontier-gen verdicts are labeled as such rather than presented with false confidence.
6. On the feature-research side (§4): the JPEG-AI three-cues paper (arXiv 2504.03191) deserves a full-text follow-up read before testing; a gated PRNU-absence experiment is cheap to try but should be expected to likely fail the real-vs-real cross-corpus rejection gate given its similarity to already-rejected noise/residual features.
